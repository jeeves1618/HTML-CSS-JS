Supply Chain Basics

  • What is it?

    A supply chain consists of all parties involved in directly or indirectly fulfilling a customer request. The supply chain includes not only the manufacturer and suppliers, but also transporters, warehouses, retailers, distributors and the customers themselves.

    We can think of supply chain as a dynamic network of entities that facilitates the constant flow of information, product, and funds between different stages.

  • Objective

    The objective of every supply chain should be to maximize the overall value generated. The value, a supply chain generates, is the difference between the value of final product to the customer and the sum of all costs incurred by the supply chain. This overall value is measured in terms of supply chain profitability or Supply Chain Surplus.

    The higher the supply chain profitability, the more successful is the supply chain.

  • Supply Chain Surplus

    Total Cost Incurred by the supply chain + Profit margin = Price of the product.

    Value of the final product to the customer - Price of the product = Consumer Surplus

    Price of the product + Consumer Surplus = Supply Chain Surplus

    The success of the supply chain should always measured in terms of supply chain surplus and not in terms of the profits at the individual stage.

    When we look for sources of value in a supply chain, we should understand that the customer is the only one who can provide positive cash flow in a supply chain.

  • Decision Phases

    During the strategy phase of supply chain management, we configure the supply chain. This is where an organization may design their distribution centers and stores in a hub and spoke model. In this phase the company commits to a structure of a supply chain for the next several years. Supply chain strategy decisions, once implemented, are expensive to alter.

    Next comes the planning phase of a supply chain. In this phase, we usually plan for a year or a quarter within the confines of the constraints established in the strategy phase. The decisions during the phase includes logistical coverage, transportation sub contracting etc..

    Next comes the operations phase. In the operations phase we think about inventory management, Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) calculation etc.. The time horizon here can be daily or weekly.

  • Cycles

    The tasks in a supply chain can be divided into a series of interfaces between two parties (supplier and buyer) called cycles. For instance, customer cycle will be an interface between retailer and the customer. Similary, replenishment cycle is the interface between distributor and the retailer. Then comes the manufacturing cycle between distributor and the manufacturer, followed by the procurement cycle between manufacturer and raw material supplier.

    Each cycle has six sub processes.

    1. Supplier stage markets the product
    2. Buyer places the order
    3. Supplier receives the order
    4. Supplier supplies the order
    5. Buyer receives the order
    6. Buyer returns reverse flows to supplier

    In these six process buyer is usually the one on the customer side of the cycle and the supplier is the one on the raw material side of the spectrum.

  • Pull and Push

    All processes in a supply chain fall into one of the two categories, depending on the timing of their execution relative to end customer demand. With pull processes, execution is initiated in response to a customer order. With push processes, execution is initiated in anticipation of customer orders.

    At the time of execution of a pull process, customer demand is known with certainty, whereas at the time of execution of a push process, demand is not known and must be forecast. Pull processes may also be referred to as reactive processes because they react to customer demand. Push processes may also be referred to as speculative processes because they respond to speculated (or forecasted) rather than actual demand. The push/pull boundary in a supply chain separates push processes from pull processes

    Push processes operate in an uncertain environment because customer demand is not yet known. Pull processes operate in an environment in which customer demand is known.

  • Macro Processes

    All supply chain processes can be classified into the following three macro processes

    1. Customer Relationship Management (CRM): All processes that focus on the interface between the firm and its customers
    2. Internal supply chain management (/SCM): All processes that are internal to the firm
    3. Supplier Relationship Management (SRM): All processes that focus on the interface between the firm and its suppliers
